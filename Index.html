OpenStreetMap Tag Classifications

A Comprehensive Analysis of Non-Building Geographic Features

Executive Summary

OpenStreetMap's non-building tag classifications form a comprehensive system for mapping transportation infrastructure, human settlements, land use, natural features, and human activities. The highway category dominates with over 214 million elements, while specialized categories like aerialway, aeroway, and railway enable precise documentation of complex infrastructure.

Key organizational principles include hierarchical classification (e.g., motorway → residential), lifecycle tagging (proposed → demolished), and dual representation approaches (separate ways vs. attributes) for features like sidewalks and cycleways.

Transportation Infrastructure

Aerialway

The aerialway category encompasses cable-propelled transportation systems essential for mountainous regions, ski resorts, and urban vertical transit. These systems are classified by mechanical operation and intended use, with detailed tagging enabling precise network modeling for tourism planning, emergency response, and accessibility analysis [13].

Aerialway Types and Applications

Cable Cars & Gondolas

Enclosed cabins on continuous or reciprocating cables for urban transit, mountain crossings, and ski resorts.

Chair Lifts

Open-air seats (single to 6-person) primarily for ski resort uphill transport.

Drag Lifts

Surface pulls including T-bars, J-bars, and platter lifts for beginner areas.

Goods Aerialways

Material transport systems for mining, logging, and construction in rugged terrain.

Key Attributes

Key attributes include capacity (persons/hour), duration, seasonal operation, lighting, and heating provisions. Stations (`aerialway=station`) and pylons (`aerialway=pylon`) complete the infrastructure mapping. Modern urban gondola implementations in Medellín, Portland, and numerous alpine cities demonstrate the category's growing importance for cost-effective vertical transportation.

Highway

The highway category is OpenStreetMap's most extensive classification system, with approximately 214 million elements globally—representing roughly 21% of all tagged OSM features [118]. This hierarchical framework enables sophisticated routing, transportation planning, and network analysis.

Road Classifications Hierarchy

Class Function Characteristics
Motorway Highest-grade, high-speed, long-distance Full access control, grade-separated, multiple lanes
Trunk Major national/regional routes Near-motorway standards, occasional at-grade intersections
Primary Main urban arterials, inter-city connectors Significant traffic volumes, regional importance
Secondary Town/village connections, urban distributors Moderate traffic, more frequent access points
Residential Property access in built-up areas Traffic calming, low speeds, direct frontage
Bridges

Elevated crossings with structure type, material, and clearance specifications

Tunnels

Underground passages with length, ventilation, and emergency facilities

Fords

At-grade water crossings with seasonal accessibility indicators

Steps

Stairways for pedestrian vertical movement with step count and handrails

Dual Representation Approaches

Approach Method Advantages Trade-offs
Separate Ways `highway=footway` + `footway=sidewalk` Precise geometry, connectivity modeling Increased data volume, maintenance complexity
Attribute Tagging `sidewalk=left/right/both/separate` Data efficiency, simpler maintenance Reduced precision, cannot represent complex configurations

Conclusion

OpenStreetMap's non-building tag classifications constitute a remarkably comprehensive system for geographic knowledge representation, spanning from global transportation networks to local community facilities, from ancient geological features to emerging mobility services.

Key Organizational Principles

  • • Functional classification for routing optimization
  • • Lifecycle tagging for temporal accuracy
  • • Dual modeling approaches for flexibility
  • • Relation-based aggregation for complex systems
  • • Multilingual naming for global accessibility
  • • External referencing for knowledge enrichment

System Evolution

The taxonomy's evolution reflects community-driven adaptation to emerging needs—electric vehicle charging infrastructure, micromobility services, pandemic-responsive facility tagging—while maintaining backward compatibility and global consistency.

For practitioners, the taxonomy offers granular precision when needed and efficient generalization when appropriate, supporting diverse use cases from humanitarian response to autonomous vehicle development. The system's strength lies in its hierarchical organization, dual representation flexibility, and extensive attribution frameworks that enable applications from basic navigation to sophisticated urban analytics.
